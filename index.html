Alan Turing

The Father of Computer Science

Alan Turing (1912-1954) was a pioneering mathematician, logician, and computer scientist whose work laid the foundation for modern computing and artificial intelligence. His contributions during World War II were pivotal in the Allied victory, and his legacy continues to inspire innovation and ethical discussions in technology.

A timeline of key events

  • 1912: Alan Turing is born on June 23 in London, England.
  • 1931: Enters King’s College, Cambridge
  • 1934: Graduates from Cambridge with a degree in mathematics.
  • 1936: Publishes “On Computable Numbers” , introducing the concept of the Turing machine.
  • 1938: Completes his PhD at Princeton University.
  • 1939: World War II begins; Turing joins the Government Code and Cypher School at Bletchley Park.
  • 1940: Turing starts working on breaking the Enigma code.
  • 1941: Develops the Bombe machine, significantly advancing codebreaking efforts.
  • 1945: WWII ends; Turing’s work at Bletchley Park becomes more widely acknowledged post-war.
  • 1950: Publishes “Computing Machinery and Intelligence” , introducing the Turing Test.
  • 1952: Turing is prosecuted for homosexuality and chooses chemical castration as an alternative to imprisonment.
  • 1954: Turing dies on June 7, under tragic circumstances.
  • 1966: The Turing Award is established, named in his honor.
  • 2013: British Prime Minister David Cameron issues an official apology for Turing’s treatment.
  • 2021: Turing appears on the new £50 note in the UK
